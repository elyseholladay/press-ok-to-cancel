WhatsApp vs iMessage • Feedback

Imagine we're building iMessage. We're like, hey, I have a great idea—let's add a progress bar so people know how fast their messages are being sent, and if they got through okay! But man, that blue progress bar can take foreevvverr—you know what I mean. It's not a bad piece of feedback to the user, but... Whatsapp uses a green checkmark to indicate that a message has been sent and another to indicate that it’s been received. What the app does brilliantly is to show you the first checkmark immediately. It may not have been fully sent, but it will probably get there, so let's go ahead and show that check. This makes the user feel like Whatsapp is really fast at sending messages.

When I worked at Bazaarvoice, we worked on consumer product rating and review software. When a user submitted a review on a product, they could add related products, like a game to a review of a Wii. This feature had super-high conversion rates but was never used. I ran some user tests, and it turned out that the search was based on the number of times the search result came up—nothing else. So if you searched for “macbook” you would get “Macbook Case for Macbook Pro 13” and Macbook Pro 15”” rather than the actual Macbook, which might appear on page five. So we built a demo and bumped search results based on the number of reviews a product has; most likely a Macbook Pro has more reviews than a case. Time to find the right product dropped from 2 minutes to :15-:45 seconds. In all of the less-than-steller cases, I think the only sin committed was not USING what they built.

don't give users unoccupied time

I read an article about the Houston airport, in which passengers were complaining that the wait to get their luggage was too long. They added more baggage people, got the bags off the planes and to the claim faster... and people still complained. Turns out, it took passengers a minute to walk from their arrival gates to baggage claim and seven more minutes to get their bags. Roughly 88 percent of their time, in other words, was spent standing around waiting for their bags. So the airport decided on a new approach: instead of reducing wait times, it moved the arrival gates and baggage claim FARTHER away from each other, so it took six times longer to get there. Complaints dropped to near zero. Occupied time (walking to baggage claim) feels shorter than unoccupied time (standing at the carousel). and the worst kind of unoccupied time? waiting for a website to load on your phone... while you are waiting for the train... while you are waiting to get to work... before you have had any coffee.

More is More?

The page with 10 results took .4 seconds. The page with 30 results took .9 seconds.

Marissa started with a story about a user test they did. They asked a group of Google searchers how many search results they wanted to see. Users asked for more, more than the ten results Google normally shows. More is more, they said. So, Marissa ran an experiment where Google increased the number of search results to thirty. Traffic and revenue from Google searchers in the experimental group dropped by 20%. Ouch. Why? Why, when users had asked for this, did they seem to hate it?
