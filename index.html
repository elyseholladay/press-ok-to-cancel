So how did we get here?

We accidentally a user experience.

As kids, my brother and I would say "I didn't mean to, it was an accident!" as an excuse. My Mom's most misery-inducing response was, "you didn't mean NOT to."

Most bad user experiences are NOT caused by evil dev teams, coding away in dark secret lairs, rubbing their hands and laughing every time someone hits "Reset" instead of "Submit" on a form—they're accidental.

Visuals • Interactivity

So what is a user experience, anyway? in terms of a website or app, these are some of the things your user is going to expect... and expect to be good: visuals—what it looks like; interactivity—how it works, if it does what they expect and quickly and isn't confusing; context and language—that the options and instructions make sense based on the flow—going through the app in a way that makes sense; consistency—both in visual styles and interactions; and of course speed.

WhatsApp vs iMessage • Feedback

Imagine we're building iMessage. We're like, hey, I have a great idea—let's add a progress bar so people know how fast their messages are being sent, and if they got through okay! But man, that blue progress bar can take foreevvverr—you know what I mean. It's not a bad piece of feedback to the user, but...

Whatsapp uses a green checkmark to indicate that a message has been sent and another to indicate that it’s been received. What the app does brilliantly is to show you the first checkmark immediately. It may not have been fully sent, but it will probably get there, so let's go ahead and show that check. This makes the user feel like Whatsapp is really fast at sending messages.

When I worked at Bazaarvoice, we built consumer product rating and review software. When someone submitted a review on a product, they could add related products, like a game to a review of a Wii. This feature had super-high conversion rates but was never used—and I suspect not by anyone who had built it, either.

I ran some user tests, and it turned out that the search was based on the number of times the search result came up—nothing else. So if you searched for “macbook” you would get “Macbook Case for Macbook Pro 13 and Macbook Pro 15 and Macbook Pro 17” rather than the actual Macbook, which might appear on page five—which users never got to.

So we built a new prototype and bumped search results based on the number of reviews a product has; most likely a Macbook Pro has more reviews than a case. Time to find the right product dropped from 2 minutes to :15-:45 seconds.

In all of the less-than-stellar cases—including mine—I think the only sin committed was not USING what they built. So what about a good example?

language, flow, & context

If you are a developer, congrats, you are also a copywriter. But when was the last time you thought about what you wrote in an app?

And before you say it's that a designer's job or whoever, when did a designer last provide you a list of say, error messages? And if they did, did it contain contextual clues about where that error message appears? If you can say yes to that, lucky you! I bet most of us can't.

“If you can go through your app and find any sentence that you’d write differently if it wasn’t generated by a computer, you are reducing the effectiveness of that text and degrading the overall experience.”

Alex Sexton

Alex Sexton wrote a great post called the UX of Language, and he says, "If you can go through your app and find any sentence that you’d write differently if it wasn’t generated by a computer, you are reducing the effectiveness of that text and degrading the overall experience"

At Square Root, our yml file of translatable text in our app was eleven hundred lines long. That's a lot of text! There were plenty of error messages in there, and many of them reused across different parts of the app. Understanding where they are used is the first step to avoiding "Press OK to Cancel" instructions; being able to have human-sounding language is even better.

phone value cannot be blank when set to primary.

“Your primary phone number is blank. Enter your number, or set a different number to primary.”

No results found.

“We didn't find anything that matches [query]. Try searching for something a little different.”

In all of these cases, we aimed for providing a little more context, and a lot more friendliness. "No results found" definitely sounds like a computer wrote it, even if it was actually a developer taking a first pass. Letting our users know we understand what they typed—and showing it again in case they typo'd it—help them not be frustrated by a dead-end search.

And what about the way your users flow through your app?

Do you know what pages they go to first? What pages lead to 404's? What do they search for or go to FROM your 404 pages? You do have a search and nav links on your 404 page, right? Getting some insight into these types of questions may really change the way you think about the language you use in your app.

Sites like crazyegg also can help you track clicks and do eye tracking. But the click history proved that it might have been more confusing: those aren't links, but people sure do love to click on them.

So back to this— I'd like to imagine that to some poor developer, this probably made sense in some way. Let's call him Steve.

Maybe Steve had a designer who told him there always had to be 2 buttons on every alert dialog, and a front-end dev who told him he had to use the common alert markup and buttons, so everything would be styled consistently.

Then it's built, and during QA, someone goes, "Hey! This dialog says "are you sure you want to cancel?" and the buttons are OK and Cancel. That doesn't make sense. I have an idea: let's add more instructions."

And thus, "Click OK to cancel the current action, or Cancel to continue" was born. All out of trying to do the right thing.

But for our friend Steve, when he was making this alert, when he looked at the code, was it clear where the buttons would end up, what they would say, and what their context was?

I'd argue that the same language concepts I talked about apply to our code, too: don't make it seem like a computer wrote it.

Is it longer? yes. but human readable HTML and CSS makes it possible for US to understand what the hell we are looking at. I really like the state classes, like .is-active, or .has-error, or .js-hidden, so that I know both WHAT the current state is and how it was applied.

I don't care what kind of naming conventions you use, if you use dashes or camelcase, if you like BEM or SMACSS or OOCSS, or anything else you made up, but I do care that you do it consistently.

How many of you have ever been searching for some code, and it ends up in a file like, 'show.html.erb'. So helpful! You can search, sure, but wouldn't it be easier to know what you're looking for instead?

It's important to name your views to match your controllers, and to match your CSS files that accompany them.

is much more clear than

that has the HTML injected in it with JS.

I'm going to talk about refactoring for just a second.

When you're mid-feature, and it's late, and you're tired, yes, it takes longer to go in and find all the places this element lives, change a classname, change the CSS, and make sure nothing broke.

So instead, our code is just a teeny bit more messy, we shrug and think we'll fix it later.. Sure you will. You and all of your coworkers doing the same thing.

Keeping your assets up to date is another way to make sure everything is consistent. The longer you wait to update your version of rails means the longer you wait to update your gems.. and at some point, if the version you have is too out of date, the more likely things are to break when you DO update, so you never do.

I really love Bower for things like CSS frameworks; Bower allows you to pull in assets like Zurb Foundation, instead of installing it separately. So instead of having to rip out a bunch of stuff when you want to update, you can update pretty seamlessly behind the scenes.

Tools like Grunt or precommit hooks built into your git flow can also help us automate manual tasks that we might otherwise skip, like strippig unused selectors, minifying files, and precompiling and gzipping assets before they're uploaded.

don't give users unoccupied time

speed

I read an article about the Houston airport, in which passengers were complaining that the wait to get their luggage was too long. They added more baggage people, got the bags off the planes and to the claim faster... and people still complained.

Turns out, it took passengers a minute to walk from their arrival gates to baggage claim and seven more minutes to get their bags. Roughly 88 percent of their time, in other words, was spent standing around waiting for their bags.

So the airport decided on a new approach: they moved the gates and baggage claim FARTHER away from each other, so it took six times longer to get there. Complaints dropped to near zero.

Occupied time (walking to baggage claim) feels shorter than unoccupied time (standing at the carousel).

and the worst kind of unoccupied time? waiting for the train schedule to load on your phone... while you are waiting for the train... while you are late to a work meeting... before you have had any coffee.

Critical Path

CSS blocks rendering; load them first
JS blocks downloads; load them last

The critical path is the path from the user following a link to the working experience.

CSS blocks rendering, so you need to deal with it right away—browsers will download all CSS, sometimes even print stylesheets, before it can render the page. And if we're waiting on the rendering, the user is waiting.

JS blocks downloads, so you need to deal with these last. Browsers block parallel downloads with JavaScript so that it doesn’t start downloading jQuery and your plugin at the same time; it should be pretty obvious that if you were to start downloading both in parallel, your plugin would arrive before jQuery would. Your script might alter the page, meaning the browser will have to deal with that before it can move on to anything else.

http://csswizardry.com/2013/01/front-end-performance-for-web-designers-and-front-end-developers/

"If the page is ugly without CSS, we can live with that, but there is no page without CSS if the browser waits and waits and takes forever to timeout, showing us a blank white page."

Syotan Stefanov

Think about this: you have an HTML page; without the HTML, there is no path really. Game over. Without images? You can live without images most of the time. Without JavaScript? hopefully you'll have a good fallback. Without webfonts? You're kidding me, I don't need no stinkin' fonts when I'm late and running to the airport and checking in for the flight on the damn phone with the spotty mobile network mobile.southwest.com wants to look like native iPhone and won't let me click until mountains of JS arrive, so no, no, we do not need webfonts.

What's left on the critical path is CSS. If the page is ugly without CSS, we can live with that, but there is no page without CSS because the browser waits and waits and takes forever to timeout showing us a blank white page.

obviously, first, the less stuff we send down the tubes, the better. when you can replace an image with CSS, that's great; if you can use 2 weights of font instead of 6, great. Refactoring your CSS to remove unused selectors is pretty trivial when you compare it to removing a JS lib or an image, but it can still be valuable.

At Square Root I wrote our original Sass architecture; after about a year and a half, I did a pretty big refactor using Susy, removing some old CSS we didn't need, and making sure that IE didn't download any of the media query wrapped styles. I cut about 50kb out just doing that, and more for IE.

Then we split the CSS out into what the homepage needed—which was about 100kb—versus what the rest of the pages and apps needed—which was about 225kb, which we could lazy-load after everything was done. Combine that with gzip and an amazon CDN, coupled with some JS refactoring, we gained almost an entire second of load time.

I firmly believe that when your code is good, you enable yourself to create a better experience for your users. If you can keep your HTML and CSS, your JS and views and controllers, all named consistently, organized, and human readable, it's easier to spot where your language is out of context, where the UI might be inconsistent, or what might need refactoring.

If you have a dev setup that encourages and allows you to find the right code, reuse it and refactor it, you are more likely to do the right thing—for yourself, your team, and ultimately your users, too.

Be one of your users •
be a copywriter • be clear • be consistent • be fast

So: occasionally look at your app through the eyes of your users, to understand what it is they experience. Looking at it on your local environment is not enough. Go to production, download the app on your phone, read your website with fresh eyes.

Think about what you are writing, and the context in which it appears. Whether it's code names, comments, or error messages, context and clarity matter. In your code, being consistent in your naming, your styles, and your workflow can help you cut down on UI bugs and find the right code fast.

And last but of course not least, ask yourself when you add code or files, "is this worth it?". Speed-test your pages.. and then go check it out like a user and see if it's acceptable.
